# Anonymized Case Patterns

These are patterns abstracted from the supplied OpenReview samples. They are heuristics, not guarantees, and contain no paper or reviewer identifiers.

## Pattern A: targeted reproduction converts a concern

A reviewer cites a concrete neighboring method. The authors acknowledge the reference, reproduce it under the existing framework, report the confirmed comparison, and add the interpretation to the final version. This is stronger than asserting that the cited method is irrelevant.

## Pattern B: one focused table supports a score update

A reviewer asks about one hyperparameter, sequence length, or deployment setting. A compact table answers exactly that question, keeps the rest of the protocol fixed, and explains both the stable region and the failure region. The follow-up then becomes a short delta reply.

## Pattern C: the AC summary carries the rebuttal

The meta-review explicitly repeats clarifications, added analyses, and commitments from the author response. This means responses should be written so an AC can quote the central resolution without reading every thread.

## Pattern D: a stable negative does not decide alone

A reviewer remains negative after a technically complete response. The authors stop debating, preserve the objective record, and let the AC weigh it against other reviews and the original contribution. Do not spend the remaining budget trying to force agreement.

## Pattern E: procedural escalation needs dates and anchors

A late or low-confidence review, a score-text mismatch, or a non-response can justify an issue report only when the venue defines that mechanism. Successful reports state timestamps, exact contradictions, missed evidence, and the concrete procedural impact. They do not speculate about intent.

## Pattern F: repeated reminders can backfire

Some samples contain multiple deadline reminders, but current venue guidance can discourage this. Treat the sample behavior as historical context only. Check the current rule snapshot and default to at most one mentor-approved reminder.

## Pattern G: more rebuttal is not always safer

Large batches of new experiments, new theory, and changed method scope can make the paper harder to verify in the current cycle. The safe response is to separate current evidence from future revision work and narrow claims where needed.

## Pattern H: answer supporters too

A positive reviewer may still have a meaningful question. Complete answers help preserve the champion's support and give the AC a defensible acceptance argument. Never assume a positive score makes that review disposable.
