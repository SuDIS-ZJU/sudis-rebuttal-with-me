# Anonymized ARR Case Patterns

These patterns were distilled from the group's successful ARR samples. They are heuristics, not acceptance guarantees, and raw PDFs are intentionally not distributed.

## Pattern A: general response plus reviewer sections

Start with a short common clarification that an AC can quote, then provide one compact section per reviewer. This reduces duplication while preserving reviewer-specific answers.

## Pattern B: compound question decomposition

When a reviewer asks several questions in one paragraph, preserve the original order and answer with `(a)`, `(b)`, `(c)`. Each subpoint should contain a direct answer, an evidence anchor, and a bounded conclusion. Do not use subpoints as decoration.

## Pattern C: factual correction with paper anchors

For a mistaken premise, state the exact correction, cite the paper section/table/line in the internal evidence ledger, explain the consequence for the reviewer's concern, and stop. Avoid language about intent or competence.

## Pattern D: direct minor add-on

When a reviewer asks for a small ablation or matched comparison, report it only after author confirmation, label its setting and limitation, and explain why it is a direct response. Do not introduce a new model or large new experimental program.

## Pattern E: non-response or score mismatch

Keep the scientific response separate from a later issue report. Preserve timestamps, exact quotes, finalization state, and the guideline or issue type. A report should ask for procedural review, not demand a score or decision.

## Pattern F: stop condition

If a reviewer remains negative after a complete factual response, record the unresolved concern once and stop repeating the argument without new evidence. ARR permits revision and resubmission, so a narrower claim or a later cycle can be safer than an unbounded response campaign.
